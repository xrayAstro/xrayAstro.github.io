Transition-edge Sensor
Introduction to Transition-edge sensor (TES)
Superconducting TES microcalorimeters operate in the transition region between the normal and superconducting states of a metal film. Of the available thermometer technologies, we picked TESs as detectors of choice since they provide the necessary sensitivity, ease of fabrication, and readout technical readiness for our requirements. We are currently fabricating Molybdenum-Copper TES arrays and working on integrating them with Gold absorbers, with a target energy resolution of < 2 eV. These will then be qualified for future sounding rocket missions to study the local 1million degree diffuse interstellar medium, which is of prime astrophysical interest to our group.
We have been developing TES sensors for an upcoming sounding rocket payload with 256 pixels with < 2 eV and a total collecting area of 2 cm2 to provide sufficient collecting area for a 300-second mission. Current fabrication effort is focused on improving quality of superconducting films, designing a repeatable lift-off process and understanding and mitigating excess noise in these detectors.
Microfabrication
We’ve adapted NIST’s hybrid additive-subtractive (hasTES) process to produce Mo/Cu bilayers with predictable critical temperatures. Current fabrication effort is focused on improving quality of superconducting films, engineering the unique absorber geometries and extensive testing of the detectors we make, while understanding and mitigating excess noise in TES detectors.
Thermometer fabrication
We have explored three different TES fabrication approaches (banks, XeF2 undercut and liftoff) and currently use a multi-step liftoff process to create the various device geometries.
Absorber fabrication
Work is currently underway to downselect various deposition processes to get the right RRR for good thermal diffusivity in the gold absorbers. The large 1 mm^2 gold absorbers need corrugations to stay upright. These absorbers have an aspect ratio similar to a sheet of typing paper held flat 1 mm above a flat surface!
Calibration
Laser pulser
We calibrate TES detectors using a 405 nm pulsed UV laser to generate a fine comb of lines 3 eV apart, all the way up to 1 keV. For a thermal detector, a hundred 3 eV photons arriving in a time short compared to the integration time of the device should look the same as a 300 eV X-ray photon. We have currently resolved peaks up to 400 photons, or ~1200 eV (Jaeckel et al 2021). Here to see the spectrum.
Position dependence
To qualify the optical calibration scheme as a direct equivalent of the more widely used fluorescent X-ray scheme, we are working on demonstrating equivalence between the two by moving a laser beam at 3K using a two-axis stick slip nanopositoner that we have built in-house. This setup should also be able to investigate position-dependence of absorbers
Shielding
TES detectors are very sensitive to external magnetic fields on the order of earth’s magnetic field. We are devising ways to sufficiently shield our TES devices for our next sounding rocket flight. The challenge arises in implementing a design that will not drastically limit our Field of View (FOV) while adhering to the weight and size constraints of our sounding rocket. We are currently working on modeling different shield geometries in COMSOL. Going forward we will focus on fabricating these shields and figuring out how to implement them before and during flight. Furthermore we are exploring different fabrication methods that might help shield our devices, like superconducting ground planes.
